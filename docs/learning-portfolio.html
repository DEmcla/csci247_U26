Learning Portfolio

Throughout the semester, you'll complete weekly activities that build your skills in evaluating technology. At the end of the semester, you'll curate your best work into a portfolio demonstrating what you've learned.

This isn't about showing perfect work. It's about showing growth, judgment, and the ability to reflect honestly on your own thinking.

What You Submit

1. Six Artifacts

Select one piece of work demonstrating each of the six course learning outcomes:

Outcome Example Artifacts
Explain how AI models work and select appropriate tools Model matching exercise, training data investigation
Apply the evaluation framework Framework application from any unit
Communicate findings clearly Findings paper, methodology audit, critique
Interpret and critique data visualizations Graph evaluation, benchmark analysis, "Make It Lie" exercise
Design and conduct controlled comparisons Reproducibility test, prompt sensitivity exercise
Evaluate source reliability Training data detective, any source evaluation work

You choose which artifacts best represent your abilities. You may revise work before including it.

2. Outcome Reflections

For each artifact, write 1-2 paragraphs addressing:

  • Why does this piece demonstrate the outcome?
  • What would you improve if you did it again?
  • What did you learn from the process?

Be specific. "I learned a lot" says nothing. "I realized I was trusting AI-generated citations without checking them" says something.

3. Synthesis Reflection

One page addressing:

  • How has your thinking about AI and technology evaluation changed?
  • Which skill was hardest for you to develop? Why?
  • If you encountered a completely new AI tool tomorrow, how would you approach evaluating it?

How It's Graded

Criterion Weight What I'm Looking For
Artifacts 40% All six outcomes represented; artifacts actually demonstrate the claimed outcome
Outcome Reflections 30% Honest self-assessment; specific observations, not generic praise
Synthesis 30% Evidence of genuine growth; clear articulation of your evaluation approach

During the Semester

  • Complete activities weekly; I'll spot-check for completion
  • You'll get feedback on a few pieces to catch major issues early
  • No detailed grading until you submit the portfolio
  • Keep all your work organized—you'll need it at the end

Timeline

  • Weeks 1-12: Complete activities, keep copies
  • Week 13: Portfolio work session; bring questions
  • Week 14: Portfolio due

A Note on Revision

You may revise any artifact before including it in your portfolio. That's the point—learning means improving. Just don't fabricate work you didn't do.
