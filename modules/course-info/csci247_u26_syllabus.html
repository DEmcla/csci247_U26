Course Description

This course teaches you to critically evaluate AI and digital tools using a transferable framework. You'll learn to ask the right questions about any technology—current or future. No coding is required, and no technical background is needed.

Important: Before Week 1, complete the Getting Started guide to set up your AI accounts and portfolio document. This takes about 2 hours and must be done before the course officially begins.

Learning Outcomes

Upon completion of this course, students will be able to:

  1. Explain how AI systems work at a conceptual level, including training data, model types, and limitations
  2. Apply a structured evaluation framework to assess any AI tool's function, data, failures, access, terms, and impact
  3. Design controlled experiments to test AI tool claims and document reproducible methodology
  4. Evaluate AI-generated visualizations, benchmarks, and media for accuracy and potential manipulation
  5. Analyze privacy, security, and ethical implications of AI tool usage
  6. Communicate technical findings clearly to both technical and non-technical audiences
  7. Adapt evaluation skills to novel AI tools encountered in academic and professional contexts

Course Schedule

Week 1: Foundations (~14 hours)

Topics: What is AI? Types of models (LLMs, image generators, classifiers). How training works conceptually. Word prediction and hallucination. Introduction to the evaluation framework.

Readings:

  • IBM: What Are Large Language Models?
  • MIT Sloan: How ChatGPT Works (video)
  • IBM: What Is AI Bias?

Activities:

  • How AI Actually Works: Learn word prediction through hands-on exercises; test AI to trigger and identify hallucinations
  • Model Matching Exercise: Match 5 tasks to appropriate AI model types
  • Training Data Detective: Investigate an AI tool's training data documentation
  • Discussion: Introduce yourself, share one AI tool you use, and reflect on how you thought AI worked before this week's readings

Due Sunday: All activities, discussion post + 2 responses

Week 2: Testing Claims (~17 hours)

Topics: Designing controlled comparisons. Documenting methodology. Why reproducibility matters. AI search vs traditional research.

Readings:

  • AIMultiple: Reproducible AI
  • Calling Bullshit: Selected syllabus modules
  • ACM Queue: GPTs and Hallucination (optional, for deeper understanding)

Activities:

  • Reproducibility Test: Same prompt to Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini—document results
  • Prompt Sensitivity: Same question, three phrasings, one model—document changes
  • AI Search vs Traditional Research: Compare AI-generated citations with database research; verify every citation
  • Framework Practice: Apply four framework components (Function, Failures, Data, Access) to a familiar tool
  • Discussion: Share surprising finding from comparison exercises

Portfolio Checkpoint 1: Cover page, working TOC, all section headings. At least one artifact started.

Due Sunday: All activities, discussion + responses, Portfolio Checkpoint 1

Week 3: Evaluation Framework Deep Dive (~18 hours)

Topics: Applying the full framework (Function, Data, Failures, Access, Terms, Impact). Structured tool evaluation.

Readings:

  • Calling Bullshit: Case studies (selected)
  • Review: Evaluation framework components

Activities:

  • AI Tool Audit (Major Assessment): Full evaluation of an unfamiliar AI tool using all six framework components
  • Discussion: What was hardest to find information about your tool? Why do you think that information was difficult to locate?

AI Tool Audit Due (25%)

Due Sunday: AI Tool Audit, discussion + responses

Week 4: Visualizations & Data (~17 hours)

Topics: How AI generates graphs. Spotting misleading visualizations. Evaluating benchmark claims. AI-generated images, audio, and video. Understanding the data-to-visualization relationship.

Readings:

  • ThoughtSpot: How to Identify Misleading Graphs
  • Nature: Is your AI benchmark lying to you?
  • Data to Visualization with Excel (course lesson)
  • ACM CHI: Misleading Beyond Visual Tricks (optional)

Activities:

  • Excel Visualization Lesson: Build charts in Excel to understand how visualization choices shape interpretation. Complete the "Make It Lie" challenge—create two charts from the same data that support opposite conclusions.
  • Graph or Garbage: Have AI generate a chart, evaluate its validity using what you learned
  • AI-Generated Media & Deepfakes: Learn to identify synthetic images, audio, and video; understand detection techniques and ethical implications
  • Benchmark BS Detection: Find a published AI benchmark, identify potential issues
  • Discussion: Share most misleading visualization you found

Portfolio Checkpoint 2: At least 3 artifacts with reflections. Table of figures if using images. Consistent formatting throughout.

Due Sunday: All activities, discussion + responses, Portfolio Checkpoint 2

Week 5: Communication & Synthesis (~16 hours)

Topics: Writing for different audiences. Methodology documentation. Professional communication.

Readings:

  • Document360: Technical Writing for Non-Technical Audiences
  • USC Libraries: Writing Methodology Sections

Activities:

  • Critique the Explanation: Evaluate an AI-generated explanation, identify issues, propose fixes
  • Methodology Audit: Review a classmate's comparison exercise—could you reproduce it?
  • Discussion: What makes technical writing clear vs. confusing?

AI Privacy & Security Audit Due (25%)

Due Sunday: AI Privacy & Security Audit, activities, discussion + responses

Week 6: Application (~18 hours)

Topics: Applying all skills to a novel tool. Demonstrating framework mastery. AI in your professional field.

Activities:

  • AI in Your Field: Connect evaluation skills to your major or career interests; research AI in your domain and apply the framework
  • Tool Adaptability Exercise: Evaluate a novel AI tool under time pressure, demonstrating all course skills. Novel tool revealed Monday. Full evaluation due by end of week.
  • Synthesis Reflection: Complete final portfolio reflection

Tool Adaptability Exercise Due (30%)

Portfolio Checkpoint 3 (Final): All 6 artifacts, all reflections, synthesis reflection. Complete, polished document.

Due Sunday: Tool Adaptability Exercise, Final Portfolio

Academic Honesty Policy

Policy link: lnk.mcla.edu/honesty

A college is a community of students and faculty interested in the search for knowledge and understanding. Essential to that search is a commitment to honesty and integrity. Honesty on the part of every college student has and always shall be an integral part of the plan of higher education at Massachusetts College of Liberal Arts. Acts of dishonesty conflict with the work and purpose of the entire College and are not merely a private matter between student and instructor.

Violations of academic honesty include but are not limited to:

  • Submitting the work of others as one's own
  • Plagiarism (as defined below)
  • Communication during an examination
  • Using crib notes in an exam, except as allowed by the instructor
  • Obtaining prior knowledge of examination questions
  • Substitution of another student in an examination
  • Altering College academic records
  • Knowingly using false statements for academic benefit
  • Collaborating on material after being directed not to collaborate
  • Forging a signature of a College official or faculty member
  • Soliciting an official signature under false pretense

Plagiarism: The academic departments of the College have varying requirements for reporting the use of sources, but certain fundamental principles for the acknowledgment of sources apply to all fields and levels of work. The use of source materials of any kind and the preparation of essays or laboratory reports must be fully and properly acknowledged. In papers or laboratory reports, students are expected to acknowledge any expression or idea that is not their own. Students submitting papers are implying that the form and content of the essays or reports, in whole and in part, represent their own work, except where clear and specific reference is made to other sources. Even if there is no conscious intention to deceive, the failure to make appropriate acknowledgment may constitute plagiarism. Any quotation—even of a phrase—must be placed in quotation marks and the precise source stated in a note or in the text; any material that is paraphrased or summarized and any ideas that are borrowed must be specifically acknowledged. A thorough reordering or rearrangement of an author's text does not release the student from these responsibilities. All sources that have been consulted in the preparation of the essay or report should be listed in the bibliography.

Upon an occurrence of alleged academic dishonesty instructors may exercise their discretion in imposing a sanction. Instructors may further file charges with the Academic Appeals Committee against students if they believe that additional sanctions would be appropriate.

AI Use Disclosure

AI is simply a tool—nothing more. I use various AI tools, including Grammarly, Microsoft Editor, ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini, to help refine years of classroom materials and textbook-based resources. These tools assist in reorganizing concepts, enhancing explanations, and transforming traditional course content into more engaging, practical lessons that align with modern industry practices.

Of course, AI can efficiently produce garbage if not properly guided. As humans, it is our responsibility to maintain oversight and quality control. It's an ongoing, iterative process, and I'm learning alongside these technologies.

I'm transparent about using AI as a collaboration tool to efficiently refine content and allow me to focus on critical thinking. I expect students to approach AI with the same intentionality; that is, students should use AI purposefully, and not as a shortcut.

AI Policy for This Course

This course is specifically about evaluating AI tools. You will be using AI extensively as part of course activities. When using AI assistance for assignments, you must clearly document your interactions and describe how the AI contributed to your work.

Your job is to critically evaluate AI output, not simply accept it. Submitting AI-generated content without your own analysis, evaluation, and reflection does not demonstrate learning and will be graded accordingly.

You are responsible for ensuring the accuracy and appropriateness of any AI-generated content you include in your work. When in doubt about appropriate AI use for a specific assignment, ask in the Q&A forum or email the instructor.

Course Success Tips

To do well:

  1. Complete pre-course setup — Don't start Week 1 without your accounts and portfolio ready
  2. Spread work across the week — Readings by Wednesday, activities by Friday, don't save everything for Sunday
  3. Plan for heavy weeks — Weeks 3, 5, and 6 have major assessments on top of regular work
  4. Document everything — Screenshots, prompts used, methodology notes—you'll need them for assessments
  5. Ask questions early — Use the Q&A forum; your classmates probably have the same question
  6. Stay curious — The best learning happens when you genuinely want to understand how things work

Common mistakes:

  1. Waiting until Sunday — In an accelerated course, one missed week is devastating
  2. Not documenting methodology — You can't write about what you didn't record
  3. Accepting AI output uncritically — The whole point is to evaluate, not accept
  4. Skipping the Getting Started guide — Week 1 assumes you've already set everything up
